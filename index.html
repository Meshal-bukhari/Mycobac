Leprosy (Hansen's Disease)

🧠 Memory Hooks

  • DR for TT: HLA-DR2 & HLA-DR3 associated with Tuberculoid Leprosy
  • DQ for LL: HLA-DQ1 associated with Lepromatous Leprosy
  • RD for PB: Rifampin + Dapsone for Paucibacillary Leprosy
  • RDC for MB: Rifampin + Dapsone + Clofazimine for Multibacillary
  • Th1 for TT: Strong Th1 response in Tuberculoid
  • Th2 for LL: Stronger Th2 response in Lepromatous

Classification & Immunology: The Spectrum

TT (Tuberculoid) Pole
  • Strong Cell-Mediated Immunity (CMI)
  • Robust Th1 response
  • Granulomas++
  • Few bacilli (paucibacillary)
  • Positive lepromin test (Mitsuda reaction)
  • HLA-DR2 & HLA-DR3 association
LL (Lepromatous) Pole
  • Weak Cell-Mediated Immunity
  • Th2 humoral response predominates
  • Lots of bacilli in macrophages (Virchow cells)
  • Negative lepromin test
  • HLA-DQ1 association

These are the in-between stages, reflecting varying levels of immunity:

  • BT: Closer to TT pole
  • BB: Middle of spectrum ("borderline borderline")
  • BL: Closer to LL pole

Thickened Nerves - Key sites to remember:

  • Facial nerve (CN7) and trigeminal nerve (CN5)
  • Ulnar nerve at the elbow
  • Common peroneal nerve at fibular head
  • Greater auricular nerve in neck
  • Posterior tibial nerve behind medial malleolus

Results in sensory and motor deficits along nerve distribution.

🧠 Diagnostic Methods

  • Skin biopsy from active margins
    • Stains: Gram, Ziehl-Neelsen, or Fite (most commonly used) → stain bright red color
    • Methenamine silver → fragmented acid-fast bacilli
    • Sudan III and Sudan IV (historically) → stain bacilli black and red
    • At least six sections must be examined before declaring samples negative
  • Scrapings of skin, lymph nodes, or nasal secretions
  • Bacilloscopy
    • Obtain sample → smear and stain → search for red rods (against a blue background)
    • Examined at 100× with oil immersion
  • Organism prevalence
    • 100% of LL • 75% of BL • 5% of TL
  • PCR → useful for paucibacillary cases
    • Performed on slit-skin smears and fresh, frozen, or paraffin-embedded skin biopsy specimens
  • IHC
    • +ve staining for the PGL-1 antigen in paucibacillary disease
  • Serology w/ Anti-PGL Abs (IgG and IgM) → for multibacillary cases
    • Classify patients, monitor response to treatment, and predict reactions
    • A marker of mycobacterial "load"
    • Elevation of anti-PGL IgM antibody levels is associated with reactions and impairment of nerve function

Importantly, after the first dose, the patient is no longer infectious to others

Leprosy Reactions

Type 1 Reaction (Reversal)
  • Immunology: Increased Th1 response. In Borderline Types
  • Timing: Before(in case of pregnancy , Stress , or other infections), during, or after treatment
  • Clinical: Existing lesions become inflamed, edematous and ulcerated
  • Neuritis: Common - nerve pain, tenderness
  • Systemic: None, unlike Type 2
  • Management: Prednisone: 40 mg per day tapered over 12 to 36 weeks, depending upon whether paucibacillary vs multibacillary
Type 2 Reaction (ENL)

Think: Immune Complex Deposition, in lepromatous

  • Immunology: Th2 response, immune complex formation
  • Timing: Common in LL and BL during MB treatment
  • Clinical: New painful erythematous nodules
  • Systemic: Fever, malaise, neuritis, iridocyclitis
  • Management: Thalidomide: for severe disease, 400 mg po daily then taper to 300 mg po daily once reaction under control followed by taper to the lowest dose without a flare; latter dose then continued 2–3 months
Lucio Phenomenon

Think: Severe Necrotizing Vasculitis, in lepromatous

  • Epidemiology: Only occurs in patients from western Mexico
  • Association: Diffuse lepromatous leprosy
  • Pathology: Necrotizing vasculitis with thrombosis
  • Clinical: Purpuric macules and ulcerative bullous lesions below the knees
  • Management: Prednisone

- The tuberculids represent a group of disorders that classically were associated with systemic TB.

- They are considered immune reactions within the skin due to hematogenous dissemination of M. tuberculosis or its antigens from a primary source, in an individual with strong antituberculous cell-mediated immunity.

- Often beginning as an immune complex-mediated reaction, they evolve into a granulomatous inflammatory response.

  • Papulonecrotic tuberculid
    • Seen most frequently in children and young adults
    • Presents with dusky red papules or papulopustules that are widely distributed in a symmetric pattern
    • Typically favor the extensor extremities and buttocks
    • Individual lesions may have central necrosis and are usually asymptomatic
    • Histopathologically, there is associated vasculitis
    • Usually there is spontaneous healing of individual lesions, often with scar formation
    • Multiple cyclic eruptions are characteristic, even after antituberculous therapy
    • Due to PLEVA, GVHD
  • Lichen scrofulosorum
    • The early lesions of lichen scrofulosorum are firm, typically perifollicular, tiny yellow-brown, tiny flat-topped papules with variable scaling
    • These asymptomatic papules are grouped in clusters, predominantly on the trunk
    • Lesions may persist for months and then disappear without scarring
    • Although it is seen in all age groups, children with active TB are most frequently affected
    • This type of tuberculid has been frequently reported after BCG vaccination and can be triggered by tuberculin testing in highly reactive patients
  • Erythema induratum (nodular vasculitis)
    • A form of lobular panniculitis that is associated with M. tuberculosis infection in a subset of affected individuals, especially in countries with a high prevalence of TB
    • Both immune complex deposition, which may play a role in the vasculitic component, and a delayed-type hypersensitivity reaction to mycobacterial antigens are thought to be involved in the pathogenesis
    • Women are most commonly affected (80-90% of patients), with peaks in incidence during early adolescence and around menopause
    • Symptoms often begin after exposure to low temperatures
    • Typically, subcutaneous nodules appear on the posterior of both legs that may progress to deep, chronic, irregular deep ulcers with undermined borders
    • Healing results in atrophic, hyperpigmented scars

Histopathology

  • Caseation necrosis (variable)

  • Often negative in paucibacillary forms (LV, tuberculids)
  • More likely positive in scrofuloderma, chancre

Culture
  • Gold standard for definitive diagnosis
  • Slow growing - inform lab to culture for Mycobacteria
PCR
  • Rapid and sensitive
  • Useful when AFB/culture negative
  • Can be used on paraffin-embedded tissue

Treatment

📌 Standard Anti-TB Therapy (ATT)

Intensive Phase (2 months): HRZE - Isoniazid, Rifampin, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol

Continuation Phase (4+ months): HR - Isoniazid, Rifampin

⚠️ Extended treatment (6-9 months) often needed for cutaneous TB

Non-Tuberculous Mycobacteria (NTM)

🧠 Common Species & Exposures

  • M. marinum: "Fish Tank Granuloma" - aquatic exposure
  • M. ulcerans: "Buruli Ulcer" - endemic water exposure
  • RGM: M. fortuitum, M. chelonae, M. abscessus - some has The ability survive sterilization procedures and to even contaminate antiseptic solutions can lead to infections in surgical patients.post-procedural, medical devices

Clinical Presentations

Species Key Features
M. marinum
  • Sporotrichoid pattern (ascending lesions along lymphatics)
  • Extremities (often hands)
  • Aquarium or swimming pool exposure
  • Grows best at 31°C (slower than other mycobacteria)
  • Treatment: clarithromycin, rifampin/ethambutol, minocycline, or TMP-SMX
M. ulcerans
  • Painless, deep, undermined ulcer
  • Starts as nodule
  • Mycolactone toxin causes tissue destruction
  • Endemic in Africa, Australia, Asia
  • Affects children <15 years old
  • HIV-infected individuals do not appear to be predisposed
  • Ulcers on extremities; can become >15 cm and extend to bones
  • Skin lesions are painless
  • No regional lymphadenopathy or systemic manifestations
  • Treatment: excision (treatment of choice), local heating, rifampin + streptomycin, and amputation
M. avium complex
  • More commonly seen in AIDS patients
  • Found in environment (water, soil, animals)
  • Pulmonary infection is most common finding
  • Skin findings w/ primary inoculation or via dissemination (pustules, ulcers on legs, nodules)
  • ↑alkaline phosphatase with disseminated disease
  • Tx: clarithromycin/azithromycin + ethambutol +/- rifampin
Rapid growing mycobacteria
(M. fortuitum, M. chelonae, M. abscessus)
  • Post-trauma or procedure history
  • Pustules, abscesses, nodules
  • Surgery, injection, tattoo sites
  • M. fortuitum in nail salon exposures
  • Rapid growing mycobacteria
  • Saprophytic organisms - lives on dead materials
  • Can get infections posttrauma/surgery or medical treatments (e.g., implant placement, liposuction, botulinum toxin)
  • Post-injection abscess is another frequent presentation
  • Skin presentations vary, but most common is inflamed subcutaneous nodules
  • Grows on routine bacterial culture media (5% sheep blood agar or chocolate agar) within 7 days
  • Consider in patients with therapy-resistant or "cold" abscesses
  • Clarithromycin is treatment of choice, but surgical treatment may be needed
M. scrofulaceum
  • Produces self-limited disease, particularly lymphadenitis
  • May cause cervical lymphadenitis with sinus formation clinically indistinguishable from scrofuloderma
  • Widely distributed in environment - found in dairy products, oysters, soil, and water
  • Found on the skin of healthy people and in cutaneous lesions of leprosy
  • Primarily affects children via inhalation or ingestion
  • May cause pulmonary disease and local lymphadenitis, primarily in submandibular and submaxillary regions
  • Usually a benign, self-limited condition with no systemic symptoms except for mild neck pain
  • Involved lymph nodes slowly enlarge over several weeks, eventually leading to ulceration and drainage with fistula formation
  • Can also produce cutaneous lesions in a sporotrichoid pattern
  • Tx: Total excision of the involved lymph nodes is treatment of choice because, in general, antibiotic therapy is unsatisfactory
M. haemophilum
  • Fastidious organism that requires ferric ions to grow
  • Requires hemin, hematin, hemoglobin, or ferric ammonium citrate
  • Primarily affects immunocompromised hosts
  • Patients treated with TNF inhibitors are at increased risk
  • Can cause cervical lymphadenitis in healthy children
  • Cutaneous infections following tattooing (including permanent make-up) and acupuncture
  • Presents as single bluish-red tender pustule, papulonodule, or plaque
  • Evolves into an abscess and/or ulcer
  • Lesions often multiple and located on the extremities, often overlying joints
  • Requires a special environment to grow, with a temperature between 30°C and 32°C
  • Tx: rifampin and clarithromycin

Diagnosis

  • Consider NTM in non-healing granulomatous lesions with relevant exposure
  • Histopathology: Granulomas, neutrophilic abscesses with RGM
  • AFB Stain: Often positive, but not always
  • Culture on specialized media: Essential for species ID and susceptibility
  • Important: Inform lab you suspect NTM! Different growth requirements
  • PCR & 16S rRNA Sequencing: Rapid identification

Treatment

📌 Macrolide-Based Regimens

Foundation: Clarithromycin or Azithromycin

Combination with:

  • Rifabutin, Ethambutol
  • Quinolones (Moxifloxacin, Ciprofloxacin)
  • Amikacin, Linezolid, Doxycycline, TMP-SMX

Other considerations:

  • Surgical excision for localized lesions
  • Prolonged treatment (3-6+ months)
  • Susceptibility testing essential for RGM

🧠 First-Line TB Drugs: HRZE

  • H: Isoniazid (INH)
  • R: Rifampin (RIF)
  • Z: Pyrazinamide (PZA)

Managing Drug Side Effects

Drug-Induced Hepatitis

Symptoms: RUQ pain, nausea, vomiting, jaundice, fatigue

Labs: Elevated LFTs (AST, ALT, bilirubin)

Management:

  1. Stop all hepatotoxic drugs
  2. After LFTs normalize, reintroduce one by one:
    1. Start with Rifampin
    2. Then add INH
    3. Then add Ethambutol
    4. PZA often avoided if severe hepatitis

⚠️ Serious Reactions Requiring Immediate Drug Discontinuation

  • SJS/TEN
  • Severe hepatitis
  • Optic neuritis
  • Severe neuropathy

Always consult ID/Pulm specialist for management!
